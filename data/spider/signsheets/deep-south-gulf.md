### Home of the Band Alabama

- at_mi: 30
- spoken: Billboard: Fort Payne, the Sock Capital of the world, and hometown of the band Alabama. They once wove one American sock in eight here, and they raised the best-selling country group of all time. Mountain music, indeed.
- describe: Fort Payne was long the Sock Capital of the World, knitting a vast share of America's socks, and it is the hometown of the band Alabama, the best-selling country band ever. The town celebrates both, with a June Jam heritage and a fan club museum.

### Mobile's Mardi Gras

- leg: mobile_al_us -> hattiesburg_ms_us
- at_mi: 3
- spoken: Billboard: Mobile, birthplace of American Mardi Gras. Not New Orleans. Here, older, since the early seventeen hundreds. The floats, the moon pies thrown from them, and the whole raucous carnival started in Mobile.
- describe: Mobile, not New Orleans, held the first American Mardi Gras, dating to the French colony of the early seventeen hundreds. Its Carnival is older and, locals insist, the original, complete with parading mystic societies and moon pies flung by the case from the floats.

### The Tallest Capitol
- treatment: billboard
- leg: baton_rouge_la_us -> gulfport_ms_us
- at_mi: 3
- spoken: Billboard: Baton Rouge. The tallest state capitol in the nation, a thirty-four-story tower Huey Long built, and where the Kingfish himself was shot dead in the marble halls he raised.
- describe: The Louisiana State Capitol in Baton Rouge, at thirty-four stories, is the tallest statehouse in the United States, an Art Deco tower championed by Governor Huey Long. Long was assassinated inside it in nineteen thirty-five; the bullet marks are pointed out to visitors.

### Selma
- treatment: landmark
- leg: montgomery_al_us -> meridian_ms_us
- at_mi: 50
- spoken: Crossing at Selma, over the Edmund Pettus Bridge, where marchers for voting rights were beaten on Bloody Sunday in nineteen sixty-five, and where the nation's conscience turned.
- describe: In Selma, the Edmund Pettus Bridge was the site of Bloody Sunday in March nineteen sixty-five, when peaceful voting-rights marchers were attacked by state troopers.
